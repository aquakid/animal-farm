Animal Farm

George Orwell's Timeless Allegory of Power and Corruption
Plot Summary Characters Themes Key Quotes

Plot Summary

Animal Farm is a masterful allegory that follows the transformation of Manor Farm into Animal Farm after the animals revolt against their human owner, Mr. Jones. Led by the pigs, the animals establish a new society based on Seven Commandments of Animalism, promising equality and prosperity for all animals.

However, as time progresses, the pigs, particularly Napoleon and his propagandist Squealer, gradually modify these commandments and assume more human-like behaviors. Through intimidation, manipulation, and propaganda, Napoleon eliminates his rival Snowball and establishes a totalitarian regime more oppressive than the human leadership it replaced.

The story culminates with the pigs becoming indistinguishable from the humans they once fought against, betraying the revolution's original principles and leaving the other animals in conditions worse than before.

Characters

Napoleon

The main antagonist who transforms from a revolutionary leader into a tyrannical dictator. Based on Joseph Stalin, he uses force and propaganda to maintain power and gradually adopts human behaviors he once condemned.

Snowball

An intelligent and passionate leader who genuinely wants to improve the animals' lives. Based on Leon Trotsky, he is eventually expelled from the farm by Napoleon's dogs and used as a scapegoat for the farm's problems.

Boxer

A loyal and incredibly strong horse whose motto "I will work harder" represents the working class's dedication. His tragic end symbolizes the betrayal of the working class by corrupt leadership.

Squealer

Napoleon's chief propagandist who uses sophisticated arguments and manipulation to justify the pigs' actions and convince the other animals to accept their increasing power.

Benjamin

The cynical donkey who recognizes the corruption but does nothing to stop it. His character represents the intellectual class who see the problems but remain passive.

Clover

A maternal and caring mare who notices the changes in the farm but struggles to articulate her concerns. She represents the puzzled working class trying to understand their deteriorating conditions.

Themes

Power and Corruption

The gradual corruption of the pigs shows how power can transform idealistic leaders into oppressive rulers, mirroring historical political revolutions.

Propaganda and Control

Through Squealer's manipulative language and the modification of the Seven Commandments, the novel demonstrates how those in power use propaganda to maintain control.

Class and Education

The pigs' intellectual superiority and the other animals' inability to read highlight how education and knowledge gaps can be exploited to maintain social hierarchies.

Notable Quotes

"All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others."
"The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which."
